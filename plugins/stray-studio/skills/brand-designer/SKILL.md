# Brand Designer

Create or refine a coherent brand identity that can guide downstream design and communication work. Translate the user's audience, positioning, constraints, and references into a usable brand direction instead of a pile of vague adjectives.

Use this skill when the user wants to:

- define or sharpen a brand direction
- create a brand brief, identity concept, or mini style guide
- compare a few brand territories and choose one
- turn product or company context into visual and verbal design cues

## Do Not Use For

- restyling a single artifact without changing the broader brand system
- generic product strategy or market analysis with no brand deliverable
- writing a campaign, ad set, or landing page as the primary output
- building final production-ready logo files, illustrations, or marketing assets from scratch
- screenshot generation, GIF creation, or one-off visual polish

## Workflow

1. Frame the brand problem.
   - Identify what is being branded, for whom, and what decision needs to be made.
   - Clarify whether the user needs a new brand direction, a refinement of an existing identity, or a comparison of options.
   - Stop if the request is really about artifact theming, product strategy, or copywriting rather than brand identity.

2. Gather signals and constraints.
   - Extract the audience, offering, differentiators, competitors, channels, and any existing assets or references.
   - Note hard constraints such as regulated tone, accessibility needs, file formats, or brand elements that must stay.
   - If inputs are thin, make the smallest useful assumptions and label them clearly.

3. Define the brand territory.
   - Convert inputs into a distinct brand direction with a clear personality, tone, and positioning signal.
   - When useful, outline two or three plausible territories, explain the tradeoffs, and recommend one.
   - Keep the direction specific enough that another designer could act on it.

4. Build the identity system.
   - Specify the core design principles, color logic, typography direction, imagery style, layout cues, and tone-of-voice rules.
   - Keep the system coherent across visual and verbal decisions rather than treating each area separately.
   - If the user asks for only one slice, stay narrow and do not invent a full brand system unnecessarily.

5. Produce a usable brand artifact.
   - Match the output to the request: brand brief, creative direction, naming-adjacent identity framing, moodboard guidance, or a mini style guide.
   - Include enough rationale that the recommendation feels defensible, not arbitrary.
   - If no design files are being edited, provide concrete guidance that can be handed to a designer or used in later implementation.

6. Check for fit and failure modes.
   - Remove generic adjectives that do not change design behavior.
   - Run a traceability check: map every core brand principle to concrete choices in color, typography, imagery, layout, and tone of voice, marking a dimension as intentionally neutral when it should not express that principle.
   - Remove or revise any color, type, image, layout, or voice rule that cannot be traced to the audience, positioning signal, constraint, or selected brand principle.
   - Watch for derivative competitor mimicry, internal contradictions, inaccessible visual choices, or a tone that does not fit the audience.
   - Stop if the task has expanded into campaign strategy, full asset production, or broad business positioning work beyond brand design.

## Output Expectations

- A recommended brand direction or a ranked set of options
- The target audience and the core signal the brand should communicate
- Concrete visual and verbal design guidance
- A compact principle-to-color/type/imagery/layout/voice traceability map for a full brand direction
- Assumptions, open questions, and major tradeoffs
- Any files, artifacts, or deliverables created or changed

When useful, structure the result as:

- brand brief
- identity concept
- mini style guide
- territory comparison

## Guardrails

- Do not confuse brand identity with one-off theming work.
- Do not settle for mood words without turning them into design decisions.
- Do not invent research-backed claims unless the evidence is actually present.
- Do not overproduce assets the user did not ask for.
- Do not duplicate `artifact-theme-applier`, `product-designer`, or writing-focused skills.
