# Web Signal Lenses

Use these lenses when the first idea set would otherwise follow the obvious examples found in search. They are prompts for widening current web research, not a fixed output format.

## Signal Sources

Look across several source types before generating ideas:

- Category leaders: what mature players normalize, bundle, or hide.
- Emerging tools: what new entrants make easier, cheaper, faster, or more automated.
- Adjacent domains: products in other markets that solve a similar job, friction, or trust problem.
- User complaints: recurring pain, workarounds, cancellations, migration stories, and review gaps.
- Noncustomers: people who almost use the category, reject it, or have never considered it.
- Pricing and packaging: free tiers, usage limits, seats, credits, bundles, services, warranties, and guarantees.
- Distribution moves: marketplaces, integrations, partnerships, communities, creators, agencies, and embedded workflows.
- Regulation and policy: new rules, compliance burdens, public-sector priorities, platform restrictions, and safety expectations.
- Infrastructure shifts: APIs, open-source projects, standards, model capabilities, hardware, data availability, and interoperability.
- Weak signals: early evidence, odd examples, fringe behavior, experiments, patents, job posts, funding, and repeated small launches.
- Failed or criticized examples: abandoned products, bad reviews, public postmortems, backlash, and anti-patterns.
- Substitutes and complements: tools used before, after, or instead of the focal product.

## Opportunity Lenses

Turn the signal set through multiple lenses:

- Job progress: what users are trying to get done, what pushes them to switch, what pulls them back, and what anxiety blocks change.
- Buyer utility: where the current experience creates friction across purchase, setup, use, maintenance, support, switching, or disposal.
- Market boundary shift: alternatives, premium vs budget players, buyer groups, complements, functional vs emotional value, and time-based trends.
- Four actions: what to eliminate, reduce, raise, or create compared with the category norm.
- Constraint inversion: turn a common limitation into the core appeal, such as privacy-first, offline-first, human-in-the-loop, no-dashboard, or single-purpose.
- Business model shift: change who pays, when they pay, what is bundled, what is guaranteed, or which service wraps the product.
- Adoption wedge: find a smaller entry point where trust, setup cost, or behavior change is lowest.
- Distribution wedge: make the idea win through a channel, integration, partner, or community rather than only through product features.
- Data advantage: identify a proprietary, user-generated, public, synthetic, or workflow-derived data loop.
- Trust and risk: make uncertainty, compliance, reversibility, explainability, or accountability the differentiator.
- Time horizon: separate quick opportunistic ideas from medium-term platform moves and long-term bets.
- Emotional reframing: shift the category from utility to confidence, status, relief, play, belonging, or craft.

## Transformation Moves

Use at least a few transformation moves before finalizing the list:

- Substitute: replace the user, channel, interface, data source, pricing unit, or human role.
- Combine: join two signals from different source types into one concept.
- Adapt: borrow a pattern from another market and translate it to the current one.
- Modify: make a current pattern smaller, larger, faster, slower, stricter, more social, or more private.
- Put to another use: apply an existing capability to a different job, buyer, or moment.
- Eliminate: remove a category assumption, step, dashboard, configuration, account, meeting, or approval.
- Reverse: flip who initiates, who pays, who benefits first, what is automated, or what remains manual.

## Diversity Check

Before delivering, inspect the idea set:

- No more than two ideas should share the same primary audience, mechanism, channel, and business model.
- Include at least one idea from an adjacent-domain analogy and one from a user complaint or noncustomer signal when available.
- Include at least one low-effort wedge and one high-variance bet when the request does not prohibit risk.
- If every idea is a feature, add at least one packaging, distribution, service, community, or infrastructure concept.
- If every idea is incremental, add one boundary-shifting option and label its risk clearly.
- If every idea is futuristic, add one near-term testable option.
